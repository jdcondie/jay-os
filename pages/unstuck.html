Start Failure
Ask: What feeling shows up when I think about starting this task?

"It won't be good enough" → Type 1: Idealistic

  • fixCommit to a bad first version. Say aloud: "I'm making the ugly version."
  • fixSet a 15-min timer. The only goal is to produce something that exists.

Fear, dread, boredom → Type 2: Avoidance

  • fixName the emotion out loud. "I'm dreading this because..." Naming creates distance.
  • fixFind the cathedral. Shift from "I have to write this report" to "I'm building a reputation for clean, fast delivery."
  • fixIf you can't find the cathedral, the task may need to be delegated or dropped.

"I don't know where to start" → Type 3: Operational

  • fixDefine the next single physical action. Not the project. The literal next move. "Open the doc." "Write one subject line."

None of these fit → Start Sideways

  • fixGive yourself permission to just peek. Open the file. Read the first paragraph. No commitment to finish. Hyperfocus often takes over once you cross the line.
Sights-Set Drill: 30s panoramic gaze → 30s narrow focus on one object → speak what you're about to do aloud → start within 60 seconds.
Drift
You were working. Now you're somewhere else. The TPN disengaged and the DMN grabbed the wheel.

Task lost novelty

  • whyFact Finder 6 already extracted the pattern. The remaining work is transcription, not creation.
  • fixFind the unsolved part. Quality edge? Structural improvement? If genuinely nothing left to solve, make it a speed challenge: "Can I finish this in 12 minutes?"

Visual alternative pulled you away

  • fixFull-screen mode. Now. Close every tab except the task. Phone in another room.
  • fixSticky Note Friction Point: Before switching context, grab a physical pen. Write your intention: "Switching to Slack to reply to Jake about the deadline." Pulls action out of autopilot into executive function.

Fuel tank drained mid-task

  • fixDon't push through. Walk 5–10 minutes. Sunlight if possible. Come back with one clear next action.
Task Displacement
"I'm productive but not on the right task." Most dangerous pattern — it feels like working.
Your attention system doesn't prioritize by importance. It prioritizes by cognitive reward. Problem solving, creation, strategy = high reward. Follow-up emails, closing loops, admin = low reward.
  • 01Name the avoided task right now. Write it on a sticky note. Make it physically visible.
  • 02Run the ownership check. Is this mine? Convert: "This deliverable is my reputation."
  • 03Shrink it. "Send the email" not "handle the client situation."
  • 04Do the avoided task first. Before opening the interesting thing. Your brain will work on the interesting thing regardless — it doesn't need protection.
  • 05If the same task keeps getting displaced day after day — that's signal. Delegate, automate, or drop it.
Finish Line Problem
You built the thing. It's 80% done. The concept is proven. Your brain wants the next challenge. The last 20% looks like maintenance.
Reframe: The finish is the next build. The case study = a build. The handoff doc = system design. The final polish = quality architecture. The launch = an experiment.
  • 01Write a one-page done doc. Define exactly what done looks like. List every remaining task.
  • 02Ask: "What am I creating by finishing this?" Not closing — creating.
  • 033-minute envision session. Close your eyes. See the project finished. Feel the relief, pride, momentum.
  • 04Declare aloud: "The thinking is done. I am now a Spender."
  • 05Set a public finish date. Tell one person. Rename the project with your name.
  • 06Write a "Wins on Completion" list. What becomes available when this ships?
  • 07Remove the "I can always come back later" escape hatch.
Option Overload
Too many tabs. Too many projects. This is pattern saturation, not distraction. Your brain generates options constantly — but possibility space creates decision fatigue.
  • 01Write down every open loop. All of them. On paper, not digital. Paper forces commitment.
  • 02Circle the one that matters most this week. Not the most interesting — the most important. Ask: "Which one, if finished, makes the others easier or irrelevant?"
  • 03Close everything else. Literally. Every tab. Every doc. One task, full-screen.
  • 04Write a "Later" list for the rest. Your brain needs to know they're captured so it stops holding them in working memory.
  • 05Set a constraint: "I'm working on this one thing until [specific time or milestone]."
The decisive question: "Which path makes commitment inevitable?" When that switch flips, your brain doesn't wander. It runs.

Motivation Formula

Motivation = (Benefit − Cost) × Likelihood of Success

When you can't move, one of these three variables is broken. Identify which one, then apply the fix.

Variable 1 Benefit is unclear
"Why does this even matter?"
Fix: Make the outcome vivid and personal. What does finishing this actually unlock for you? Feel it — don't just think it.
Variable 2 Cost feels too high
"This is going to take forever."
Fix: Shrink the task. You don't have to do the whole thing. Just the first 5 minutes. Lower the cost until starting feels smaller than not starting.
Variable 3 Low belief in success
"I'll probably fail anyway."
Fix: Create a smaller version where success is almost guaranteed. Stack wins until the belief shifts. Momentum changes evidence.
